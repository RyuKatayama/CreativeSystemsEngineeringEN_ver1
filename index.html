Background: Limitations of Traditional Systems Engineering

Problem Statement: Limitations of Traditional SE

Traditional Systems Engineering

Underlying Assumption: The System-of-Interest is “given”.

Customer Requirements
→ System-of-Interest (already known / pre-given)
→ Design & Implementation

Problems

  • It never asks “What should we treat as the system?”
  • The processes of creation and discovery are missing.
  • It is hard to apply directly to innovation.

Creative Systems Engineering (CSE)

Underlying Assumption: The System-of-Interest is “created”.

?
→ System Discovery & Creation (Phase 0)
→ Traditional SE (realization phases)

Core Question

How can we identify, specify, and create a “system that does not yet exist”?

Definition and Positioning of Creative Systems Engineering

Definition

Creative Systems Engineering (CSE) is a system of theories, processes, and methods for discovering and creating systems that do not yet exist.

Relationship with Traditional SE

Perspective Traditional SE CSE
Primary Question How to realize the given system? What system should we create?
System Given / already known Object of discovery and creation
Starting Point Requirements Definition Stakeholders & Concerns
Focus Realization Discovery + Realization

Application Domains of CSE

  • Development of new products and new services
  • Creation of new businesses
  • Discovery of new technologies
  • Exploration of scientific research topics
  • Design of policies and institutions
  • Solving complex social problems

Significance of CSE

CSE expands the scope of systems engineering from “system realization” to “system creation”.

Theoretical Foundations: Four Pillars

∫ Category Theory

  • Formalizes system discovery as a functor.
  • Provides a mathematical description of structures through objects, morphisms, and composition.

◆ IEEE 42010

  • Provides the structural relationship among Stakeholders, Concerns, and Systems.
  • International standard framework for architecture description.

⚙ Conceptual Engineering

  • Design, implementation, and evaluation of concepts.
  • Normative approach to improving concepts via continuous refinement cycles.

≡ Equivalence Transformation Theory

  • Provides an essential process model of creation.
  • A Japanese-origin theory of creativity proposed by Dr. Kamekichi Ichikawa (1955).

Significance of Integration

Re-interpret and develop Equivalence Transformation Theory (1955) within modern theoretical frameworks.

CSE = Equivalence Transformation Theory × Category Theory × IEEE 42010 × Conceptual Engineering

Category-Theoretic Model of System Discovery

Three Categories and Functors

  • C_Stakeholder (Category of Stakeholders)
    Objects: Stakeholders
    Morphisms: Relations of interest, cooperation, conflict
  • C_Concern (Category of Concerns)
    Objects: Concerns
    Morphisms: Dependency relations, contradictions
  • C_System (Category of Systems)
    Objects: Systems
    Morphisms: Inclusion, evolution, substitution, dependency

Auxiliary functors:

  • H (“has”): from stakeholders to concerns
  • G (“satisfies”): from systems to concerns

Core functor for system discovery:

F: C_Stakeholder × C_Concern → C_System
F(s, c) = “the system that satisfies concern c of stakeholder s”.

Category-Theoretic Interpretation of Equivalence Transformation

We consider:

  • Source system Aο (existing system) with additions Σa
  • Target system Bτ (new system) with additions Σb
  • Category of essence C_essence (Cε): universal structures
  • Forgetful functor U (from concrete systems to essence)
  • Free construction functor F (from essence back to concrete systems)

Equivalence Transformation = Composition of Functors
Equivalence Transformation = F ∘ U : C_source → C_target

In other words: first “forget” (extract the essence), then “freely reconstruct” (re-concretize) a new system.

Three Approaches to System Discovery

A. Concern-Driven Approach

Starting Point: Unmet concerns.

Stakeholders × Concerns
↓
Discovery of unmet concerns
↓
System-of-Interest

Example:

Unmet concern of elderly people: “freedom of mobility”
→ Mobility system for elderly people

B. Equivalence-Transformation Approach

Starting Point: Essence (Cε) of an existing system.

Analysis of an existing system A
↓
Extraction of the essence (Cε)
↓
New system B

Example:

Essence of Uber = “matching idle resources with demand”
→ Airbnb-like systems in other domains

C. Boundary-Redefinition Approach

Starting Point: Current system boundary.

Existing system boundary
↓
Questioning and re-framing the boundary
↓
Redefined system

Example:

Expanding the boundary of “automobile”
→ MaaS (Mobility as a Service)

Category-Theoretic Views

  • Concern-driven approach: a functor from C_Concern to C_System.
  • Equivalence-transformation approach: functors inside C_System.
  • Boundary-redefinition approach: expansion / contraction of subcategories within C_System.

Generative Architecture Framework (GAF)

Extension of IEEE 42010

Current IEEE 42010

System (given)
→ has Architecture
→ described by Architecture Description
→ composed of Models
Views, Viewpoints, and Concerns frame and structure these descriptions.

GAF: Extension of IEEE 42010

Newly added: Generation Phase

Stakeholder Universe
→ has Concern Universe
→ generates System Hypotheses
→ evaluated by Generative Viewpoints
→ recorded in System Genesis Records
→ System-of-Interest
→ (then handed over to the conventional IEEE 42010 process)

Core Idea of GAF

Make explicit the “pre-phase” of IEEE 42010, and record how the System-of-Interest has been discovered and created, so that the generative process becomes traceable and reproducible.

New Concepts Introduced by GAF

1. Stakeholder Universe

The total set of potential stakeholders, including:

  • Direct Stakeholders
  • Indirect Stakeholders
  • Future Stakeholders
  • Extreme Stakeholders
  • Anti-Stakeholders (opponents)

2. Concern Universe

The total set of potential concerns, including:

  • Functional / Quality Concerns
  • Economic / Temporal Concerns
  • Social / Emotional Concerns
  • Latent Concerns

3. System Hypothesis

A hypothesis that “such-and-such a system ought to exist”.

  • Target stakeholders and concerns
  • Value proposition (hypothesized provided value)
  • System boundary hypothesis
  • Transformation rationale

4. Generative Viewpoint

A viewpoint for discovering and generating systems.

  • Concern-Driven Viewpoint
  • Equivalence-Transformation Viewpoint
  • Boundary-Redefinition Viewpoint
  • Analogical / Negation Viewpoint

5. System Genesis Record

A record of how a system has been discovered and created, to ensure reproducibility and traceability of the discovery process.

Phase 0: System Discovery Process

The New Front-End Process for Creative SE

0.1 Universe Exploration

Comprehensive exploration of potential stakeholders and concerns, and creation of a bird’s-eye map of existing systems.

0.2 Opportunity Detection

Identification of unmet concerns, discovery of contradictions among stakeholders, and detection of “white spaces” in the system landscape.

0.3 Hypothesis Generation

Generation of multiple system hypotheses using concern-driven, equivalence-transformation, and boundary-redefinition approaches.

0.4 Hypothesis Evaluation

Evaluation of system hypotheses using:

  • Conceptual engineering criteria: explanatory, epistemic, practical, and moral adequacy
  • Business / economic evaluation

0.5 System Selection

Selection of the most promising system hypotheses, and creation of System Genesis Records.

Phase 0: System Discovery
→ System-of-Interest Definition
→ Phase 1–N: Conventional SE

Significance of Phase 0

Phase 0 makes the previously implicit “system identification” step explicit and formalized, thereby improving the reproducibility and teachability of creative activities.

Application Domains of Creative Systems Engineering

New Product Development

  • Focus: discovering latent consumer concerns
  • Approaches: concern-driven, analogical reasoning
  • Key evaluation: market potential, emotional adequacy

New Service Development

  • Focus: redefining experiential value
  • Approaches: equivalence transformation, boundary redefinition
  • Key evaluation: scalability

New Business Development

  • Focus: equivalence transformation of the essence of existing businesses
  • Approaches: boundary redefinition, equivalence transformation
  • Key evaluation: consistency of business models

New Technology Development

  • Focus: analogy from nature and other domains
  • Approach: functorial analogy (e.g., biomimetics)
  • Key evaluation: technical novelty, patentability

Exploration of Scientific Themes

  • Focus: structural correspondence between theories
  • Approach: search for natural transformations, discovery of universal structures
  • Characteristic question: “Where does the analogy break?” = source of new research problems

Policy and Institution Design

  • Focus: coordination among diverse stakeholders
  • Approaches: concern-driven, transformation of foreign case studies
  • Key evaluation: moral and practical adequacy

Common Framework Across Domains

Phase 0.1: Universe Exploration
→ Phase 0.2: Opportunity Detection
→ Phase 0.3: Hypothesis Generation
→ Phase 0.4: Evaluation & Selection

Each domain specializes its focus, generative approaches, and evaluation criteria within this common framework.

Conclusion: Significance of Creative Systems Engineering

Essence of CSE

While traditional SE focuses on “how to realize the given system”, CSE addresses the question “what should we create as a system in the first place?”.

Theoretical Contributions

  • Revitalizes a Japanese-origin theory of creativity — Equivalence Transformation Theory — within modern theoretical frameworks.
  • Bridges category theory, standards, and conceptual engineering.

Practical Contributions

  • Improves the reproducibility of creative activities.
  • Enhances the teachability and learnability of creative system design.

Social Contributions

  • Provides a systematic methodology for innovation to industry, academia, and policy-making.

CSE = Category Theory (formalization) × IEEE 42010 (systematization) × Conceptual Engineering (social implementation) × Equivalence Transformation Theory (essence of creation)
